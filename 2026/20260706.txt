2026_07_06 | The grammar of outrage
↳ [back to full archive](2026/2026_full-archive.html)
↳ [back to index](index.html)

Empathy evolved for the immediate and the concentrated: a face, a recognizable story, a moment of peak harm followed by some kind of resolution. It works on what you can point to. This is why a photograph of one child moves more than statistics on thousands — not callousness, but structure.

Slow violence doesn't have that shape. A displacement crisis that unfolds across a decade, an environmental catastrophe that accumulates across generations — there's no single worst day, no clear before-and-after, no moment that frames the story. The suffering is real and continuous, but it doesn't fit the narrative grammar that activates response. Compassion fatigue sets in not because people stop caring but because the mechanism isn't built for this.

!!This feature of certain kinds of harm — that they can be made diffuse enough to escape the [quality of attention](post.html?p=2026/20260421.txt) that would otherwise make them legible — is sometimes deliberate.!!
