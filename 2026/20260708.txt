Stoicism [isn't about numbness](post.html?p=2026/20260210.txt) — but it's often read as calling for distance anyway: care less, hold things lightly, and you'll suffer less. Epictetus corrects this with a specific example. In the Discourses, he rebukes a father who fled his sick daughter because he couldn't bear the anxiety of watching her suffer. Leaving, Epictetus argues, isn't Stoic restraint. It's weakness.

What Stoicism actually asks is harder: stay fully present to what you love, but locate that love in something other than fear of loss. The anxiety of possession — the clinging that turns love into dread — is what creates suffering. Not the love itself. !!Remove the anxiety, not the attachment.!!
