In his memoir *A Confession*, Tolstoy describes arriving at a point where the recognition that everything passes — people, achievement, health, life itself — made all pleasure drain. He couldn't unfeel what he'd seen. Joy became preemptive mourning. His solution was to take on faith what he couldn't hold by reason.

Dōgen, the 13th-century Buddhist philosopher, looked at the same fact and drew the opposite conclusion. Transience is what makes beauty possible. !!His image: moonlight in dewdrops shaken from a crane's bill — [beautiful precisely because it won't stay](post.html?p=2026/20260630.txt).!! Happiness isn't a state to reach and hold. It's a fire to relight.
