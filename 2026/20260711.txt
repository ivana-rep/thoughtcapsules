Sartre's existentialism left the individual in a kind of radical solitude: you are free, you define yourself through your choices, and other people are mostly a problem — the famous line is "hell is other people." The freedom is real but it's lonely, and its ethics are hard to derive.

De Beauvoir, writing in *The Ethics of Ambiguity*, pushed the logic further. If freedom is the highest good — if that's what it means to be human — then wanting it for yourself commits you logically to wanting it for others. My freedom can't be the foundation of my ethics while I treat someone else's as a variable to manage. !!You can't genuinely want to be free while working to keep someone else from being free.!!
